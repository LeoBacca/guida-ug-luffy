When it comes to the playstyle of the deck, **Blue/Green Luffy** is a very aggressive deck that relies heavily on value generation and board control.

I think what a lot of people fail to understand with this deck is that you are not a glass cannon. Instead, you are a sustained value engine that can generate a very high number of cards throughout the game.

A lot of players get too focused on trying to aggro immediately with this deck, without realizing that they can first build a strong board, and only then start swinging with everything together to put real pressure on the opponent.

In my opinion, the best way to play this deck is to develop as many characters as possible, especially in the early game. You want to go as wide as possible while keeping your characters standing, and then start attacking with them all at once.

Most decks in the meta cannot deal very well with extremely wide boards, because there are not many real board wipes. Some players try to remove them with cards like 5-cost sweepers, but most of the time that is not enough, since every small character we generate has usually already cycled itself.

The most important thing with this deck is understanding your objective in each matchup. Most of the time, your goal is to be as wide as possible and create several attacks in the same turn. Having multiple swings is usually the best way to win the game.

In general, the deck wants to develop reliable bodies that can stick for multiple turns, mainly the 8k swingers, while also developing smaller characters that often work as one-shot attackers. Even if those smaller bodies get removed, they usually force at least one attack from the opponent, slowing them down and buying us time.

When playing the deck, you should focus on creating sustained damage while keeping your board as efficient as possible. You want to avoid turns where you expose your entire board and lose everything at once.

Your turns should usually combine both development and aggro. Since the deck has many ways to rebuild the board, there will be situations where you can expose a large part of your board, knowing that the following turn you can rebuild it while slowing your opponent down through cheap effects, Blockers, or simply by drawing enough cards to deny their aggression.

I also tend to stay as healthy as possible with this deck. Having 4 life is often very dangerous against many decks in the meta, but since this deck draws a lot of cards, countering early attacks and preserving life is usually a very strong strategy.

By staying healthy, you can bait your opponent into attacking your board instead. As long as they are forced to go into your board, you are gaining time to keep drawing cards, slowly trimming their resources, and continuing to pressure them at the same time.

## 4.1 The Mulligan

The mulligan for **Blue/Green Luffy** is usually quite simple, because we have many cards that we want to see in the early game. Most of the time, these are our 2-cost characters and 1-cost Luffy.

These cards are the key to our early game.

When it comes to the mulligan, the most important thing is making sure that we can develop several bodies in the first two turns of the game. Of course, this changes depending on whether we are going first or second.

Going second is usually the preferred curve for this deck, because we play more even-cost characters. The best opener is often to develop two of our 1-cost or 2-cost characters, since we can already re-stand our DON!! on turn one with our Leader effect.

Most of the time, we want to look for Mr.1, Mr.2, 1-cost Luffy, and, in this version, You Can Be My Samurai.

The reason why we want You Can Be My Samurai is that, in many games, we can start with two of these characters and immediately play Samurai on them, especially since most opponents will not be able to trade into our board right away. Of course, this depends on the matchup, but in general these are the cards we want to see in the early game.

If we talk about a generic mulligan, I would say the 1-cost Monkey D. Luffy is one of the best cards to look for, because he can basically search for almost everything in the deck. From there, we want to find another card that allows us to continue our curve.

After that, Mr.1 and Mr.2 are always among the best starts, no matter which curve we are playing. Even if we have both in hand, we can play one, re-stand the DON!!, and then play the other one to develop an early board while increasing our consistency and seeing more cards in the first stages of the game.

Of course, there is a difference between going first and going second with this deck, but generally speaking, once you reach the 5 DON!! turn, the deck becomes quite open.

For the opening hand, however, it is very important to understand what your best early plays are.

As already mentioned, developing board in the early game is extremely important. In order to achieve this, having access to our 4-cost characters when going second is one of the best things we can have.

Especially Crocodile paired with any 2-cost character is one of the strongest starts. You can develop a 2-cost character on your first turn, then on the 4 DON!! turn play Crocodile, bounce back that card in order to re-stand into Crocodile efficiently.

If you play 1-cost Luffy in this sequence, you can also use You Can Be My Samurai by resting Crocodile and Luffy. This does expose them, but most of the time your opponent will not be able to trade into Crocodile efficiently. Many decks want to play on curve, and they often cannot keep up for more than one trade into Crocodile at that stage of the game.

As mentioned before, it is very important to bait your opponent into attacking your board, especially when you have 8k characters. For this reason, playing an early You Can Be My Samurai can be very strong in many situations.

When it comes to Ivankov, he is one of the best cards to go wide and search for follow-ups. Normally I prefer starting with Crocodile, especially when going second, but Ivankov can sometimes be our curve.

For example, if we miss a 2-cost character on the first turn, we can play Ivankov, search for a 2-cost character, and play it immediately. If we draw into something else, we can also use the two extra DON!! from our Leader effect to develop another card.

This is usually one of the best ways to establish an early board.

Ivankov is not the best character for building board in terms of raw stats, because he is not a very strong body to leave on the board. However, he still cycles himself and searches for the next play, so I do not mind playing him. He can find the missing pieces we need, whether that is a 2-cost character, 1-cost Luffy, a 5-cost card, or even cards we will need for the late game.

Usually, when going second, you want to prioritize Crocodile over Ivankov if you already have a 2-cost character on the board. However, if you are missing follow-up plays for the next turns, especially for the 8 DON!! turn, Ivankov is usually better.

Of course, both Crocodile and Ivankov become much stronger when paired with the 1-cost and 2-cost cards we already mentioned above.

### Odd curve good stuff
> 🖼️ *"Odd curve good stuff" — see source screenshot for chapter 4 in `IMG/`.*

When it comes to the odd curve, I think one of the best cards you can see is 5-cost Buggy, usually paired together with Prisoner of Impel Down.

Of course, you do not need to mulligan specifically for Prisoner, but it is always good to have one. Since we are running 12 copies, we should usually see one by the 5 DON!! turn.

You can also see that I kept Crocodile in the odd curve mulligan. The reason is that Crocodile is very strong going first when paired with any 2-cost character.

On this 5 DON!! turn, we can attach one DON!! to one of our 2-cost characters, either Mr.1 or Mr.2, swing with it, then play Crocodile, bounce it back, and replay it standing. This makes it much harder for the opponent to trade into it.

Because of that, Crocodile is a very nice follow-up on the 5 DON!! turn.

5-cost Buggy is also one of the best cards we can play in 5 DON!!, especially against Leaders that can remove our board, or against bigger Leaders like Nami and Ace. I think Buggy is one of the best cards to play on curve in those situations.

Of course, you do not need to hard mulligan for Buggy, but having this card in your opening hand is usually very nice, since we have a lot of consistency in seeing at least one Prisoner of Impel Down.

The last card is Boa Hancock, which is usually very strong when played on the 7 DON!! turn. After playing Boa Hancock, we can always re-stand two DON!! with our Leader effect and use those DON!! either to attach to our characters and swing, or to develop another 2-cost body to help set up the win.

I think the odd curve is usually the best one when we are playing into a deck that wants to go first.

One of the best things about this deck, as we mentioned before, is that we do not have a fixed curve. Because of that, we have to always adapt to the opponent's curve, and as you can see, we can also pair our even-cost cards very well with the odd curve.

## 4.2 The early game

The early game with **Blue/Green Luffy** is, of course, one of the strongest parts of the deck.

The reason is simple: we can develop a lot of board very quickly, and not many decks can properly deal with that.

As we already mentioned in the mulligan section, we can have very strong openers depending on our curve. Most of the time, having multiple 2-cost characters paired with 1-cost Luffy and our 4-cost characters is the ideal start.

Our early game should always be focused on developing board. I think what a lot of people fail to understand is that you need to build sustained board presence while slowly poking your opponent, instead of committing immediately to full aggression.

Even though the deck presents itself as an aggro deck, and it is definitely an aggro deck, you still need to understand that many of your bodies are going to be fragile. Because of that, you usually want to build the board first, and only start resting your characters when you actually want to force your opponent to swing into them.

Your early turns should be focused on finding the best way to develop a wide board.

Especially between the 4 DON!! and 6 DON!! turns, the best lines usually involve developing 2-cost bodies, re-standing our DON!!, playing a 4-cost character, and sometimes developing another 2-cost character in the same turn.

One of the best openers, as mentioned before, is on 2 DON!!: play a 2-cost character, either Mr.1 or Mr.2, re-stand our two DON!!, play 1-cost Luffy, and then play You Can Be My Samurai to draw two extra cards.

If you do not have You Can Be My Samurai, most of the time you can simply play two 2-cost characters, either Mr.1 or Mr.2. After that, on the 4 DON!! or 6 DON!! turn, you can develop one of your 4-cost or 5-cost characters.

I usually like developing 5-cost Buggy over my 4-cost character when I am playing against a deck that can remove our board easily. This applies to decks that can run cards like Gamma Knife, decks that can efficiently clear our small bodies, or decks with heavy swingers in the early game.

That being said, most of the time I do not mind resting my 8k characters, because many decks need to play on curve and will not have enough attack power to trade into those cards efficiently.

Also, having many cards in hand is quite common with this deck, so countering early attacks on our 8k bodies is usually easy.

Most of the time, forcing the opponent to swing into our 1-cost or 2-cost bodies is already good for us, especially if they do not have a developed board. If they need to commit a 5k swing or even more to remove characters that already cycled themselves and attacked at least once, we are gaining value.

The objective of the early game is to build a resilient board that can generate value and then be used to attack all at once when we enter the mid game.

Especially, entering the 5 DON!! turn with 5-cost Buggy plus Prisoner of Impel Down, and then combining them with cards like You Can Be My Samurai, will be a big problem for many decks. Most opponents will not be able to trade into two 6k bodies at the same time that early in the game.

## 4.3 The mid game

The mid game with this deck should always be focused on starting to set up lethal.

The most important thing is that we do not give up our board development during this stage of the game. From the 6 DON!! to 10 DON!! turns, we have access to several combos, and we can start chaining multiple cards together.

Having the ability to re-stand two DON!! with our Leader effect allows us to push a lot of damage while still developing our board. We can also bounce back our pieces with Crocodile and replay them to avoid having them traded into.

At the same time, we can also assign most of our DON!! to swing with the whole board, then re-stand two DON!! and develop new characters afterward. This forces our opponent to trade into our board, while we keep grinding their resources by making them chase characters that have already cycled themselves.

The key in this part of the game is understanding what kind of game we are playing.

As mentioned before, I always recommend most of our DON!! to swing, but **Blue/Green Luffy** is a deck that can apply sustained damage while generating resources. It can still win even if the game reaches the late stages, which makes the mid game very solid and flexible.

I will not give you a fixed blueprint for this part of the game, because the deck has many different lines. However, most of the time, one of the strongest things you can do is develop Boa Hancock and understand whether you can pair her with another piece, or simply use Boa Hancock together with DON!! assignment to pressure your opponent.

In most matchups, Boa Hancock is going to be central during this stage of the game.

Sometimes, instead of relying on Boa Hancock, you can simply go wide by playing 5-cost Buggy plus Prisoner of Impel Down, then freeze something with Mr.3. Another possible line is developing Buggy plus Prisoner, then playing Crocodile to bounce back one of your characters and reestablish multiple bodies on the board.

These types of turns can easily leave you with several 6k bodies on the board, which will help you a lot in the later stages of the game.

What matters the most in the mid game is understanding how much time is left in the game and trying to enter the late game with a strong and resilient board setup. This will allow us to reach a position where we can use our re-stand Event to set up powerful lethal turns.

## 4.4 The late game

When it comes to the late game, the most important thing is understanding how much time you have left in the game and how to create your best lethal setup.

Of course, this always depends on the matchup you are facing, but most of the time you can prepare your swings based on your opponent's Leader power.

If you are a 5k Leader, I usually like to make several 8k swings; against bigger Leaders, like Nami for example, you usually want to attack with more 7k and 5k swings.

From there, you need to start thinking about the best way to enable your re-stand Event.

Most of the time, you will be able to set it up by having one re-stand from turns, then redeveloping your board and ending with three or four standing characters. Sometimes, you can also expose one of your bodies if it is only a 6k character, as long as you know that you can rebuild or complete the setup later.

When you need to go for lethal, you can often play another differently named character for one or two DON!!, swing with your entire board, then use Let's Go to the Navy Headquarters to re-stand your characters and finish the game.

That being said, you do not always need the re-stand Event to win. Some games can be closed naturally through Boa Hancock, or simply by having enough pressure on the opponent and using your existing board to finish them off.

The key in the late game is recognizing when you are supposed to commit everything and when you should keep developing one more turn before going for the final push.

## Conclusion

Overall, I think it is very important to understand the general mechanics of the deck, but of course you will also need to study the small details in order to truly master every matchup.

**Blue/Green Luffy** is one of those decks that really rewards player skill and understanding of the opponent's deck. It is a very skill-intensive deck that will constantly test your fundamentals.

For this reason, I always recommend studying the fundamentals behind the deck first, and then trying to master them as you understand which details matter the most in each matchup.

In general, the deck is quite intuitive, but there is a big difference between simply understanding how to play it and actually mastering it. Once you understand where the value really lies, you can get the best out of this deck and make every matchup winnable.

No matchup is going to be completely free, but if you are skilled enough, every matchup can be won. Overall, I do not think this deck has any truly bad matchups.
